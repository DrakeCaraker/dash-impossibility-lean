\begin{abstract}
Attribution instability is not a bug in SHAP---it is a mathematical property of attribution itself, at every level of a model.
We prove that no attribution ranking can simultaneously be faithful, stable, and complete under the Rashomon property (the Attribution Impossibility); for binary questions, faithful + stable alone is impossible (the bilemma). The theorem applies to \emph{any} attribution system: input-level (which feature matters) and component-level (which circuit computes).
\textbf{Input-level:} 50 retrains produce 24 distinct top-3 rankings (4.2\% agreement); 45\% of German Credit applicants receive different explanation categories; 68\% of 77 datasets show instability. A 7-line nonparametric diagnostic outperforms the Gaussian formula by $2\times$.
\textbf{Component-level:} 10 independently trained transformers on TinyStories agree at only $\rho{=}0.54$ on circuit importance by activation patching. A $G$-invariant projection (orbit averaging over the architectural symmetry group) lifts agreement to $\rho{=}0.98$, with 14/14 theory-derived predictions confirmed across two scales. A fine-tuned GPT-2 confirms the boundary: without Rashomon diversity, instability vanishes ($\rho{=}0.993$).
The design space has exactly two achievable families; \DASH{} (orbit averaging) is Pareto-optimal. Verified in Lean~4 (357 theorems, 6 axioms, 0~\texttt{sorry}).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Train a gradient-boosted model. Compute SHAP values. Retrain with a different random seed. The ``most important feature'' changes---in 68\% of 77 public datasets.
Now look inside a neural network. Identify the most important circuit component by activation patching. Retrain from a different initialization. The most important component changes too---10 independently trained transformers on TinyStories agree at only $\rho = 0.54$.

The instability is not in SHAP, not in activation patching, and not in any specific method. It is in the mathematics of attribution itself. We prove that \emph{no} attribution ranking---at any level of a model, from input features to internal circuits---can simultaneously be faithful, stable, and complete when the Rashomon property holds. The same theorem, the same resolution, the same diagnostic, at different granularities.

\paragraph{Two levels, one impossibility.}
Consider \emph{input-level attribution}: across 30 retrains, 45\% of German Credit applicants receive a different ``most important feature'' category. The adverse action notice changes based solely on the training seed.
Now consider \emph{component-level attribution}: across 10 independently trained transformers, all 4 attention heads in layer~0 appear as ``most important'' under different seeds---full $S_4$ symmetry realized. A circuit analysis depends on which equivalent network you happened to train.

The reason is the same: collinear features (or architecturally symmetric components) create a Rashomon set where equivalent models attribute importance in opposite orders. The fix is the same: \emph{orbit averaging}---aggregate across the symmetry to recover what is shared. For features, this is \DASH{} (ensemble averaging); for circuits, this is the $G$-invariant projection. Both are the Reynolds operator on different symmetry groups.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{The Attribution Impossibility} (Theorem~\ref{thm:impossibility}): no attribution ranking is faithful, stable, and complete---at any granularity. Zero axiom dependencies. The \textbf{bilemma} (Theorem~\ref{thm:bilemma}): for binary attribution questions, faithful + stable alone is impossible.
    \item \textbf{The Design Space Theorem} (Theorem~\ref{thm:design-space}): exactly two families of methods exist. \DASH{} (orbit averaging) is Pareto-optimal.
    \item \textbf{Component-level validation}: 14/14 theory-derived predictions confirmed on TinyStories at two architectural scales. $G$-invariant projection lifts circuit agreement from $\rho{=}0.54$ to $\rho{=}0.98$. GPT-2 fine-tune confirms the boundary: without Rashomon diversity, instability drops to near-zero ($\rho{=}0.993$, flip $= 0.043$).
    \item \textbf{Input-level validation}: 45\% explanation reversal, 24 distinct top-3 rankings on the canonical SHAP tutorial dataset, nonparametric diagnostic outperforming the Gaussian formula by $2\times$.
    \item \textbf{Machine-verified proof}: 357 Lean~4 theorems from 6 axioms, 0~\texttt{sorry}---the first formally verified impossibility in explainable AI.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Setup and the Attribution Impossibility}
\label{sec:setup}

An \emph{attribution system} consists of a set of configurations $\Theta$ (trained models, circuits, or any parameterized explananda), an attribution function $\varphi_j(\theta) \geq 0$ measuring the importance of component $j$ in configuration $\theta$, and a ranking $\succ$ on the components.

\begin{definition}[Faithful, Stable, Complete]
\label{def:desiderata}
$\succ$ is \emph{faithful} to $\theta$ if $j \succ k$ whenever $\varphi_j(\theta) > \varphi_k(\theta)$;
\emph{stable} if $\succ$ does not depend on $\theta$;
\emph{complete} if for every $j \neq k$, either $j \succ k$ or $k \succ j$.
\end{definition}

The \emph{Rashomon property} \citep{rudin2024amazing}: for interchangeable components $j, k$, there exist configurations $\theta, \theta'$ with $\varphi_j(\theta) > \varphi_k(\theta)$ and $\varphi_k(\theta') > \varphi_j(\theta')$. It is inevitable for any stochastic algorithm whose training distribution is invariant under permutation of symmetric components (Rashomon Inevitability Theorem, Lean-verified; supplement). Deterministic algorithms with fixed tie-breaking rules can break this symmetry.

\begin{theorem}[Attribution Impossibility]
\label{thm:impossibility}
If an attribution system satisfies the Rashomon property, no ranking is simultaneously faithful, stable, and complete.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $j, k$ be interchangeable. Rashomon gives $\theta, \theta'$ with opposite orderings. Suppose $\succ$ is faithful, stable, complete. Completeness: WLOG $j \succ k$. Stability: this holds for all configurations. Faithfulness at $\theta'$: requires $k \succ j$. Contradiction. \hfill Lean: \texttt{explanation\_impossibility}, zero domain axioms.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Instance 1: Input-level attribution.}
$\Theta$ = trained models; $\varphi_j$ = global SHAP importance of feature $j$; interchangeability = collinearity ($\rho > 0$).
$P$ features are partitioned into $L$ groups by correlation structure.
The attribution ratio diverges as $1/(1{-}\rho^2)$ for \GBDT{}, is $\infty$ for Lasso, conditional for neural nets, and $O(1/\sqrt{T})$ for random forests (supplement).

\paragraph{Instance 2: Component-level attribution.}
$\Theta$ = independently trained networks; $\varphi_j$ = importance of component $j$ (head, MLP block) by activation patching; interchangeability = the loss landscape has $S_k^L$ symmetry (permuting heads within a layer yields an equivalent minimum). After training, heads specialize---but different seeds realize different permutations of these specializations.
The Rashomon property holds empirically: all 4 heads in layer~0 of TinyStories transformers appear as ``most important'' across 10 seeds.

\begin{theorem}[Design Space]
\label{thm:design-space}
The achievable set of $(S, U, C)$ triples consists of exactly two families:
\textbf{Family~$\mathcal{A}$} (single-configuration): $U = 1/2$, $C = \text{complete}$;
\textbf{Family~$\mathcal{B}$} (orbit averaging): $U = 0$, $C = \text{ties}$, $S = 1 - O(1/M)$.
No third family exists among deterministic aggregation methods (Figure~\ref{fig:design-space}). $(S{=}1, U{=}0, C{=}\text{complete})$ is infeasible. \DASH{} is Pareto-optimal among unbiased aggregations on the $\mathcal{B}$ branch.
\end{theorem}

\section{The Bilemma and Diagnostics}
\label{sec:bilemma}

\subsection{The Bilemma}

For \emph{binary} attribution questions---``positive or negative?'', ``selected or not?'', ``in the circuit or not?''---the impossibility is strictly stronger.

\begin{theorem}[The Bilemma]
\label{thm:bilemma}
For binary $H$ with $\mathrm{incompatible}(h_1, h_2) \Leftrightarrow h_1 \neq h_2$, no method is simultaneously faithful and stable under the Rashomon property. Completeness is not needed.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Binary $H$ has no neutral element: every value contradicts the other. Faithful forces $E(\theta) = \mathrm{explain}(\theta)$, making $E$ decisive. The trilemma gives $\bot$. \hfill Lean: \texttt{bilemma\_of\_compatible\_eq}, zero axioms.
\end{proof}

The bilemma applies to input-level binary questions (SHAP sign, feature selection) and to component-level binary questions (``is this head part of the circuit?''). It is robust to perturbation: for binary $H$, $\varepsilon$-stability reduces to exact equality, so the bilemma holds at every tolerance level (Lean: \texttt{ApproximateBilemma.lean}). For continuous $H$, the quantitative bilemma gives: unfaithfulness $\geq (\Delta - \delta)/2$ for any $(\varepsilon,\delta)$-stable method on a Rashomon pair with gap $\Delta$ (supplement).

\paragraph{Tightness classification.}
$F{+}S$ is achievable iff $H$ has a \emph{neutral element} (Lean: \texttt{tightness\_dichotomy}). Rankings have ties (neutral); SHAP signs and circuit membership do not. The bilemma has \emph{collapsed} tightness---structurally more severe than the fairness impossibility \citep{chouldechova2017fair}. The only resolution is enrichment: adding neutral elements, which is precisely what orbit averaging does.

\subsection{Diagnostics}

\paragraph{Input-level: nonparametric flip predictor.}
For feature $j$ across $M$ models, the \emph{minority fraction}
$\hat{p}_j = \min(\#\{m : \varphi_j(f_m) > 0\},\; \#\{m : \varphi_j(f_m) \leq 0\}) / M$
directly estimates the flip rate. It achieves Spearman $0.92$--$0.98$ across all datasets (Table~\ref{tab:cc-comparison}), outperforming the Gaussian formula ($0.46$--$0.89$). The complete predictor is 7 lines:

{\small
\begin{verbatim}
def predict_sign_instability(shap_matrix):
    signs = np.sign(shap_matrix)  # M models × P features
    n_pos = (signs > 0).sum(axis=0)
    n_neg = (signs < 0).sum(axis=0)
    total = n_pos + n_neg
    return np.where(total > 0,
        np.minimum(n_pos, n_neg) / total, 0)
\end{verbatim}
}

The variance $\Var_f[\varphi_j(f)]$ across the Rashomon set equals the minimum MSE any stable method achieves; \DASH{} saturates it exactly (0/800 violations). \paragraph{Component-level: symmetry group diagnostic.}
For neural network components, the architectural symmetry group $G = S_k^L$ (permutations of $k$ heads within each of $L$ layers) determines which components are interchangeable. The $G$-invariant importance $V^G_c = \frac{1}{|G_c|}\sum_{g \in G_c} \varphi_{g(c)}$ averages importance within orbits, separating stable structure (layer-level, head-vs-MLP) from unstable permutation (which specific head within a layer). This is the component-level analogue of the minority fraction: it identifies \emph{which} attributions are trustworthy before running multiple models.

\section{Resolution: Orbit Averaging}
\label{sec:resolution}

The impossibility at both levels admits the same resolution: \emph{average over the symmetry group}. This is orbit averaging (the Reynolds operator $R_G[\varphi]_j = \frac{1}{|G_j|}\sum_{g \in G_j} \varphi_{g(j)}$), applied to different groups at each level. The mathematical content is elementary---the power is in identifying \emph{which} group to average over and \emph{why} this is the minimal sufficient aggregation.

\paragraph{Input-level: \DASH{}.}
\DASH{} (\textbf{D}iversified \textbf{A}ggregation for \textbf{S}table \textbf{H}ypotheses) averages $|\SHAP|$ values across $M$ independently trained models. It changes explanations, not predictions.

\begin{corollary}[\DASH{} equity]
\label{cor:equity}
For a balanced ensemble, $\bar\varphi_j = \bar\varphi_k$ for features $j, k$ in the same group.
\end{corollary}

\DASH{} is \emph{uniquely} optimal for binary $H$ (majority vote) and achieves the variance lower bound $\sigma_j^2/M$ with equality for continuous $H$ (Cauchy--Schwarz; Lean: \texttt{sum\_squares\_ge\_inv\_M}). By Rao--Blackwell, \DASH{} (the sufficient-statistic-based estimator for exchangeable models) minimizes MSE among unbiased estimators.
Ensemble size: $M_{\min} = \lceil 2.71 \cdot \sigma_{jk}^2/\Delta_{jk}^2 \rceil$ for 5\% between-group flip rate; $M = 25$ brings flips below 1\%.

\paragraph{Component-level: $G$-invariant projection.}
For a network with symmetry group $G = S_k^L$, the $G$-invariant importance $V^G$ averages activation patching scores within orbits (heads in the same layer). For $S_k^L$ this reduces to layer averaging---a familiar operation, but the theorem identifies \emph{why} it works (it is the unique orbit average for the architectural symmetry group) and \emph{when} it suffices (when $G$ captures the interchangeability). For richer symmetry groups (e.g., weight permutation symmetries), $V^G$ extends beyond simple layer averaging. On TinyStories: pairwise Spearman rises from $\rho = 0.54$ (raw) to $\rho = 0.98$ ($G$-invariant; Section~\ref{sec:component-experiments}).

\paragraph{The same mathematics.}
Both resolutions are orbit averaging: \DASH{} averages attributions across models (trivially equivariant under $S_M$), while $V^G$ averages importance within architecturally symmetric components (equivariant under $S_k^L$). The substantive content is the $V^G$ construction: it identifies the \emph{minimal sufficient} aggregation from the symmetry group alone, without requiring multiple models. Both sacrifice completeness (report ties for interchangeable components) to gain stability. Both are Pareto-optimal on the $\mathcal{B}$ branch of the Design Space (Theorem~\ref{thm:design-space}).

\paragraph{Workflow.}
Screen (single model) $\to$ Minority fraction (nonparametric) $\to$ Z-test (5 models) $\to$ \DASH{} consensus ($M \geq 25$). For circuits: compute $V^G$ from architectural symmetry; no retraining needed.

\subsection{Component-Level Attribution}
\label{sec:component-experiments}

Raw importance agreement is low ($\rho = 0.565$, $0.540$)---independently trained networks disagree substantially on which head is most important, exactly as the impossibility predicts. Within-layer flip rates are $0.496$ and $0.489$, indistinguishable from the theoretical coin-flip prediction of $0.50$. Head-vs-MLP flip rate is $0.000$ across all 30 models: the stable between-orbit structure is perfectly preserved. All 4 heads in layer~0 of Config~A appear as ``most important'' across 10 seeds---the full symmetric group $S_4$ is realized.

The $G$-invariant projection lifts agreement to $\rho = 0.972$ and $0.982$---the residual stable structure (layer importance, head-vs-MLP) is nearly identical across independently trained networks. Split-half reliability ($0.991$, $0.960$) vastly exceeds between-model agreement ($0.565$, $0.540$), confirming that within-model measurements are precise but between-model variation is structural. Random projections of heads to the same dimensionality achieve only $\rho = 0.30$--$0.41$ (vs.\ $0.97$--$0.98$ for $V^G$); the $G$-invariant projection ranks at the 100th percentile of random projections (permutation $p < 0.001$).

\paragraph{GPT-2 boundary condition.}
A fine-tuned GPT-2 (same task, 10 seeds) shows $\rho = 0.993$ raw and within-layer flip $= 0.043$ (vs.\ ${\sim}0.50$ for from-scratch training). Fine-tuning does not create a diverse Rashomon set---it barely perturbs the pre-trained circuits. The theorem correctly predicts: \emph{less Rashomon diversity $\Rightarrow$ less instability}. The near-zero residual flip rate ($4.3\%$ vs.\ ${\sim}50\%$) confirms that the theory is falsifiable and the boundary holds.

\paragraph{Method robustness.}
Mean ablation (an alternative to weight zeroing) yields $G$-invariant $\rho \approx 0.97$--$0.99$ at both scales. Cross-method per-model agreement is only $\rho \approx 0.5$--$0.6$: the two ablation methods disagree on which specific head matters, but agree on $V^G$. The disagreement between methods is itself an instance of the impossibility---different ablation ``seeds'' produce different raw attributions, but the orbit-averaged structure is invariant.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\citet{bilodeau2024impossibility} prove completeness + linearity cannot coexist---a complementary axis (method properties vs.\ cross-model stability). The results are orthogonal: uncorrelated features with a linear SHAP method satisfy Bilodeau's impossibility but not ours ($\rho = 0 \Rightarrow$ no Rashomon); a nonlinear method on correlated features satisfies ours but not Bilodeau's (no linearity assumed). \citet{huang2024failings} show SHAP can misrank features; \citet{srinivas2019full} prove complete attributions cannot be weakly input-dependent; \citet{rao2025limits} establishes Kolmogorov complexity barriers. None address stability, quantitative bounds, or constructive resolution.
\citet{fisher2019models} compute variable importance ranges across Rashomon sets; \citet{laberge2023partial} find partial-order rankings. Our theorem proves partial ordering is a mathematical necessity, not a pragmatic choice.
\citet{damour2022underspecification} document that underspecification causes instability across domains empirically; we prove the impossibility that underspecification entails for attribution specifically.
\citet{krishna2022disagreement} document that different explanation methods disagree for the same model; we prove the complementary result that the \emph{same} method disagrees across equivalent models.
\citet{chughtai2023toy} show that independently trained networks learn different internal representations for the same group operation---empirical Rashomon for circuits. Our component-level results extend this: the instability in circuit importance is mathematically inevitable, and orbit averaging resolves it.
\citet{chouldechova2017fair} and \citet{kleinberg2017inherent} prove fairness trilemmas; ours is the explainability analogue.
\citet{nipkow2009social} formalized Arrow in Isabelle/HOL; \citet{zhang2026statistical} formalized statistical learning in Lean~4; ours is the first formally verified impossibility in XAI. Extended discussion in supplement.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The split-count axioms assume full signal capture ($\alpha = 1$; for finite-depth trees $\alpha \approx 2/\pi$, $R^2 = 0.89$); the impossibility holds for any $\alpha > 0$.
The equicorrelation assumption simplifies the axioms; the Rashomon property holds pairwise.
\DASH{} requires $M \times$ training cost ($M = 25$ for $<$1\% flip rate).
The 68\% prevalence figure is from benchmark-representative datasets and may not generalize to production pipelines with decorrelated features.
The component-level validation uses weight zeroing and mean ablation; other patching methods (path patching, causal scrubbing) may show different instability patterns, though the theorem predicts the same structure.

\paragraph{Broader implications.}
The impossibility operates at every level of a model. For input-level attribution, single-model SHAP audits for proxy discrimination conclude ``proxy reliance'' with probability exactly $1/2$ for collinear features; for $K$ protected attributes, joint audit correctness is $(1/2)^K$. We suggest this may constitute a ``known and foreseeable circumstance'' under EU AI Act Art.~13(3)(b)(ii)~\citep{euaiact2024}; legal scholars should evaluate this interpretation.
For component-level attribution, the impossibility constrains \emph{importance rankings} of circuit components: which head matters most depends on the training seed (Section~\ref{sec:component-experiments}: Cohen's $d = 5.4$--$11.9$). This does not necessarily constrain \emph{algorithmic identification}---whether a specific computational function (e.g., induction) exists may be stable even when the specific head implementing it is not. Safety cases built on ``does function $F$ exist?'' may be robust; safety cases built on ``which component is most important?'' are subject to the impossibility.
Sparse autoencoder (SAE) features decompose a fixed model's activations into interpretable directions not tied to $S_k^L$. Whether SAE-based attribution escapes the Rashomon property---because the decomposition is post-hoc on a fixed model---is an open question with implications for the interpretability agenda.
The resolution at both levels is orbit averaging, which recovers the shared structure across the Rashomon set.

\paragraph{Open problems.}
Scaling the component-level analysis to frontier models (GPT-4 scale); extending the $G$-invariant projection to non-architectural symmetries (learned representations). Formalizing the Bayes-optimal half of the design space in Lean (requires measure-theoretic decision theory). The gap between prediction and explanation is structural at every level---developing practical workflows that acknowledge this gap, rather than assuming it away, is the central challenge for trustworthy AI.
