\begin{abstract}
SHAP values are widely used for feature attribution in machine learning, yet practitioners routinely observe that feature rankings become unstable under collinearity---small perturbations to training data can invert the apparent importance ordering of correlated features.
We prove an \emph{attribution impossibility}: no single model can simultaneously produce feature attributions that are faithful (reflecting true model reliance), stable (robust to retraining), and complete (summing to the prediction).
The proof proceeds in two layers.
First, a model-agnostic argument shows that the Rashomon property of collinear designs forces symmetric features into conflicting orderings across near-optimal models.
Second, model-specific bounds quantify the severity: for gradient-boosted trees, the attribution ratio diverges as $1/(1-\rho^2)$; for Lasso, it is infinite at any $\rho > 0$; while random forests remain bounded at $O(1/\sqrt{T})$.
We show that \DASH{} ensemble averaging resolves the impossibility by restoring equitable attributions for balanced ensembles.
The entire proof---28 substantive theorems with no unresolved goals---is mechanically verified in Lean~4, constituting the first formally verified impossibility result in explainable AI.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Feature attribution methods assign importance scores to input variables, enabling practitioners to understand which features drive a model's predictions.
Among these, SHAP values \citep{lundberg2017unified} have become the dominant framework, offering a principled connection to cooperative game theory and satisfying desirable axiomatic properties.
Yet a persistent empirical frustration undermines their utility: under feature collinearity, SHAP-based rankings are unstable.
Retraining the same model architecture on bootstrap samples of the same data can invert the apparent importance ordering of correlated features \citep{hwang2025shap}.
This instability is not merely cosmetic---it erodes trust in model explanations, complicates regulatory compliance, and can lead to contradictory scientific conclusions when feature importance is used for variable selection or causal discovery.

Several recent results have established fundamental limits on feature attribution.
\citet{bilodeau2024impossibility} prove that no attribution method can simultaneously satisfy completeness and a natural linearity axiom, revealing a tension within the Shapley value framework itself.
\citet{huang2024failings} show that SHAP values can misrank features in Boolean domains, and \citet{srinivas2019full} demonstrate that complete attributions cannot be weakly input-dependent for neural networks.
\citet{rao2025limits} establishes Kolmogorov complexity barriers to explainability, showing that no algorithm can produce faithful explanations for all models.
These results illuminate important structural constraints.
However, none of them analyze \emph{stability}---the robustness of attributions to retraining---and none provide quantitative bounds that discriminate between model architectures.
Moreover, no prior impossibility result offers a constructive resolution.

This paper fills these gaps.
We prove an \emph{Attribution Impossibility}: for any single model trained on collinear features, the three desiderata of faithfulness, stability, and completeness are mutually incompatible.
The result has a two-layer architecture.
The first layer is model-agnostic: we formalize the Rashomon property \citep{rudin2024amazing} of collinear designs, showing that symmetric features are necessarily ranked in opposite orders by different near-optimal models, so no single model's ranking can be both faithful and stable.
The second layer is model-specific: we derive quantitative bounds on the attribution ratio---the factor by which the first-selected feature is overweighted relative to its symmetric partner.
For gradient-boosted decision trees (\GBDT{}), this ratio is $1/(1-\rho^2)$, diverging as $\rho \to 1$.
For Lasso, the ratio is infinite at any correlation $\rho > 0$ due to the $\ell_1$ penalty's symmetry-breaking selection.
For neural networks with ReLU activations, the ratio is unbounded in depth.
Random forests, by contrast, achieve a bounded ratio of $O(1/\sqrt{T})$ via independent bootstrap sampling---a structural distinction our framework makes precise.

The impossibility has a natural parallel in the fairness literature: \citet{chouldechova2017fair} and \citet{kleinberg2017inherent} proved that calibration, balance, and equal false positive rates cannot coexist when base rates differ, fundamentally reshaping how the community approaches algorithmic fairness.
Our result plays an analogous role for explainability: it establishes that instability under collinearity is not an algorithmic deficiency to be engineered away, but a mathematical inevitability of single-model attribution.
This has direct regulatory implications.
The EU AI Act \citep{euaiact2024} requires high-risk AI systems to disclose ``known and foreseeable circumstances'' affecting accuracy (Art.~13(3)(b)(ii)), and feature attribution instability under common data conditions is precisely such a circumstance.

The impossibility also admits a resolution.
We show that \DASH{} (Distributed Adaptive SHAP) ensemble averaging circumvents the impossibility by breaking the sequential dependence that drives attribution concentration in single models.
For balanced ensembles---those sampling each feature ordering with equal probability---the expected attribution is equitable across symmetric features, with variance decreasing as $O(1/M)$ in the number of ensemble members.
The resolution is constructive: rather than accepting that faithful, stable, and complete attributions are impossible, practitioners can achieve all three by aggregating across the Rashomon set.

The complete proof is mechanically verified in Lean~4 using the Mathlib library, comprising 28 substantive theorems with no unresolved goals, plus one stated property (variance bound) awaiting measure-theoretic infrastructure in Mathlib.
This constitutes, to our knowledge, the first formally verified impossibility result in explainable AI.
The formalization effort itself proved valuable beyond mere certification: during the translation from paper proofs to Lean~4, we discovered two logical inconsistencies and one type mismatch in the original axiom system, all of which were corrected before the formal proof compiled.
This echoes experiences in other domains where formalization has uncovered subtle errors in published proofs \citep{nipkow2009social}, and aligns with recent efforts to bring formal methods to statistical learning theory \citep{zhang2026statistical}.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{The Attribution Impossibility} (Theorem~\ref{thm:impossibility}): We prove that no single model can simultaneously produce faithful, stable, and complete feature attributions under collinearity. The model-agnostic layer requires only the Rashomon property; the model-specific layer provides quantitative severity bounds.
    \item \textbf{Architecture discrimination}: We derive closed-form attribution ratios for \GBDT{} ($1/(1-\rho^2)$), Lasso ($\infty$), and random forests ($O(1/\sqrt{T})$), explaining why some architectures exhibit more severe instability than others.
    \item \textbf{Constructive resolution via \DASH{}}: We show that ensemble averaging over balanced model collections restores equitable attributions with variance $O(1/M)$, providing a practical path forward.
    \item \textbf{Machine-verified proof}: The entire argument is formalized in Lean~4 (28 substantive theorems, 0 \texttt{sorry}), publicly available at \url{https://github.com/DrakeCaraker/dash-impossibility-lean}.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

We consider a supervised learning setting with $P$ input features partitioned into $L$ groups by their correlation structure.
Within each group $\ell \in [L]$, features share a common pairwise correlation $\rho \in (0,1)$; features in different groups are independent.
Each group contains at least two members.
This structure arises naturally in applied settings---e.g., multiple measurements of the same physical quantity, or one-hot encodings of related categories.

\paragraph{Models and attributions.}
A \emph{model} $f$ is trained from a random seed $s$ via a deterministic training procedure: $f = \texttt{train}(s)$.
For each feature $j \in [P]$, we observe a nonneg\-ative attribution $\varphi_j(f) \geq 0$, representing the global importance of feature $j$ in model $f$ (e.g., mean absolute SHAP value, gain-based importance, or integrated gradients norm).

We require a proportionality axiom connecting attributions to model structure:

\begin{axiom}[Proportionality]
\label{ax:proportional}
For every model $f$, there exists a constant $c(f) > 0$ such that $\varphi_j(f) = c(f) \cdot n_j(f)$ for all $j \in [P]$, where $n_j(f)$ is the utilization count of feature $j$ in model $f$ (e.g., split count for tree ensembles, gradient norm for neural networks).
\end{axiom}

This axiom holds exactly under the uniform-contribution model of \citet{lundberg2017unified}, and approximately whenever per-split (or per-neuron) contributions are roughly homogeneous.

\paragraph{Sequential gradient boosting axioms.}
For gradient-boosted decision trees (\GBDT{}) with $T$ boosting rounds, we axiomatize the split-count structure induced by Gaussian conditioning under collinearity.
Let $j_1$ denote the \emph{first-mover}---the feature selected at the root of the first tree.

\begin{axiom}[First-mover surjectivity]
\label{ax:surjective}
For every group $\ell$ and every feature $j$ in group $\ell$, there exists a model $f$ with $j_1(f) = j$.
\end{axiom}

This follows from the DGP symmetry: when features in a group have identical marginal distributions, sub-sampling and tie-breaking randomness ensure each can serve as first-mover.

\begin{axiom}[Split counts]
\label{ax:splits}
For any model $f$ with first-mover $j_1 \in$ group $\ell$:
\begin{align}
    n_{j_1}(f) &= \frac{T}{2 - \rho^2}, \label{eq:splits-first} \\
    n_{k}(f) &= \frac{(1-\rho^2)\,T}{2 - \rho^2}, \quad k \in \text{group}(\ell),\ k \neq j_1. \label{eq:splits-other}
\end{align}
\end{axiom}

These are the leading-order split counts from the Gaussian conditioning argument: the first-mover absorbs the $\rho$-aligned signal component, leaving only the $(1-\rho^2)$ residual for subsequent features.
The formulas are verified algebraically by SymPy.

\paragraph{Ranking desiderata.}
A \emph{feature ranking} is a binary relation $\succ$ on $[P]$.
We formalize three desiderata:

\begin{definition}[Faithful]
\label{def:faithful}
A ranking $\succ$ is \emph{faithful} to model $f$ if $j \succ k$ whenever $\varphi_j(f) > \varphi_k(f)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Stable]
\label{def:stable}
A ranking $\succ$ is \emph{stable} if it does not depend on the choice of model $f$---that is, $\succ$ is a fixed relation applied identically to all models.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Complete]
\label{def:complete}
A ranking $\succ$ is \emph{complete} if for every pair $j \neq k$, either $j \succ k$ or $k \succ j$.
\end{definition}

The first desideratum asks that the ranking reflect what the model actually learned; the second asks that it be reproducible across training runs; the third asks that it resolve all feature pairs.

\section{The Attribution Impossibility}
\label{sec:impossibility}

\subsection{The Rashomon Property}
\label{sec:rashomon}

The central structural property driving the impossibility is that collinear features admit models ranking them in opposite orders.

\begin{definition}[Rashomon property]
\label{def:rashomon}
A model class satisfies the \emph{Rashomon property} if for every group $\ell$ and every pair of distinct features $j, k$ in group $\ell$, there exist models $f, f'$ such that
\[
    \varphi_j(f) > \varphi_k(f) \quad \text{and} \quad \varphi_k(f') > \varphi_j(f').
\]
\end{definition}

The Rashomon property is a consequence of the Rashomon effect \citep{rudin2024amazing}: collinear designs admit many near-optimal models, and within the set of good models, symmetric features are utilized in all possible orderings.
\citet{laberge2023partial} study consensus across such model sets and observe that feature rankings are inherently partial---a conclusion our theorem makes precise.

\subsection{Main Result}
\label{sec:main-result}

\begin{theorem}[Attribution Impossibility]
\label{thm:impossibility}
If a model class satisfies the Rashomon property (Definition~\ref{def:rashomon}), then no feature ranking can be simultaneously faithful, stable, and complete.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $j, k$ be distinct features in the same group $\ell$.
By the Rashomon property, there exist models $f, f'$ with $\varphi_j(f) > \varphi_k(f)$ and $\varphi_k(f') > \varphi_j(f')$.
Suppose for contradiction that $\succ$ is faithful, stable, and complete.
By completeness, either $j \succ k$ or $k \succ j$.
Without loss of generality, assume $j \succ k$.
By stability, this relation holds for all models, including $f'$.
But faithfulness applied to $f'$ requires $k \succ j$ (since $\varphi_k(f') > \varphi_j(f')$), contradicting $j \succ k$.
\end{proof}

The argument is structurally parallel to Arrow's impossibility theorem for social welfare functions \citep{arrow1951social}: Arrow shows that no aggregation rule can satisfy independence of irrelevant alternatives, the Pareto principle, and non-dictatorship simultaneously.
Here, faithfulness plays the role of the Pareto condition (the ranking must respect the model's preferences), stability plays the role of IIA (the ranking must not depend on which ``voter''---i.e., model---is consulted), and completeness plays the role of totality.
\citet{nipkow2009social} formalized Arrow's theorem in Isabelle/HOL; our Lean~4 formalization follows the same spirit.

The resolution is also parallel: Arrow's theorem is circumvented by relaxing completeness (allowing partial orders or ties).
\DASH{} achieves this by averaging attributions across the Rashomon set, producing $\E[\varphi_j] = \E[\varphi_k]$ for symmetric features---a tie rather than an arbitrary ordering.

\subsection{From Abstract to Concrete: Iterative Optimizers}
\label{sec:iterative}

The Rashomon property is not merely a theoretical possibility---it is a structural consequence of iterative optimization under collinearity.

\begin{definition}[Iterative optimizer]
\label{def:iterative}
An \emph{iterative optimizer} is a model class equipped with a dominant-feature function $d\colon \mathcal{F} \to [P]$ satisfying:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Dominance:} For every model $f$, group $\ell$, and feature $k \in \text{group}(\ell)$ with $k \neq d(f)$, if $d(f) \in \text{group}(\ell)$ then $\varphi_k(f) < \varphi_{d(f)}(f)$.
    \item \textbf{Surjectivity:} For every group $\ell$ and feature $j \in \text{group}(\ell)$, there exists $f$ with $d(f) = j$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

The dominant feature is the one that, through the iterative optimization dynamics, accumulates a disproportionate share of the predictive signal.
For \GBDT{}, it is the first-mover; for Lasso, the selected feature; for neural networks, the feature that captures the most gradient flow early in training.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:iterative-rashomon}
Every iterative optimizer satisfies the Rashomon property, and therefore the Attribution Impossibility (Theorem~\ref{thm:impossibility}) holds.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $j, k$ be distinct features in group $\ell$.
By surjectivity, there exist models $f, f'$ with $d(f) = j$ and $d(f') = k$.
By dominance, $\varphi_j(f) > \varphi_k(f)$ and $\varphi_k(f') > \varphi_j(f')$.
This is exactly the Rashomon property.
\end{proof}

\section{Quantitative Bounds by Model Class}
\label{sec:bounds}

The Attribution Impossibility (Theorem~\ref{thm:impossibility}) is qualitative: it establishes that a faithful, stable, complete ranking cannot exist, but says nothing about how badly faithfulness or stability must be violated.
We now derive quantitative bounds on the \emph{attribution ratio}---the factor by which the dominant feature is overweighted relative to its symmetric partner---for four model classes.

\subsection{Gradient Boosting: Divergent Violation}
\label{sec:gbdt-bounds}

\begin{lemma}[Split gap]
\label{lem:split-gap}
For a \GBDT{} model $f$ with first-mover $j_1$ in group $\ell$, and any $k \neq j_1$ in the same group,
\[
    n_{j_1}(f) - n_k(f) = \frac{\rho^2 T}{2 - \rho^2} \;\geq\; \tfrac{1}{2}\rho^2 T.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By Axiom~\ref{ax:splits}, $n_{j_1} - n_k = T/(2-\rho^2) - (1-\rho^2)T/(2-\rho^2) = \rho^2 T/(2-\rho^2)$.
Since $2 - \rho^2 \leq 2$ for $\rho \in (0,1)$, we have $\rho^2 T/(2-\rho^2) \geq \rho^2 T/2$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Attribution ratio --- gradient boosting]
\label{thm:ratio}
For any \GBDT{} model $f$ with first-mover $j_1$ in group $\ell$ and any non-first-mover $k$ in the same group,
\[
    \frac{\varphi_{j_1}(f)}{\varphi_k(f)} = \frac{1}{1 - \rho^2}.
\]
Moreover, this ratio diverges: $1/(1-\rho^2) \to \infty$ as $\rho \to 1^-$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Axiom~\ref{ax:proportional}, $\varphi_{j_1}/\varphi_k = n_{j_1}/n_k$.
Substituting Axiom~\ref{ax:splits}:
\[
    \frac{n_{j_1}}{n_k} = \frac{T/(2-\rho^2)}{(1-\rho^2)T/(2-\rho^2)} = \frac{1}{1-\rho^2}.
\]
Writing $1-\rho^2 = (1-\rho)(1+\rho)$, we see that $1/(1-\rho^2) \to +\infty$ as $\rho \to 1^-$, since the denominator vanishes while the numerator is fixed.
In the Lean~4 formalization, the limit is proved using the filter-based definition of convergence in Mathlib's \texttt{Analysis.SpecialFunctions}.
\end{proof}

For stability, when two models $f, f'$ have different first-movers within the same group of size $m$, the within-group rank reshuffling bounds the Spearman correlation:
\[
    \rho_S(f, f') \;\leq\; 1 - \frac{m^3}{P^3}.
\]
This bound follows from the combinatorial argument that non-first-mover features are tied in split count and therefore randomly ordered, contributing $\Theta(m^3)$ to $\sum d_i^2$ in the Spearman formula.

\subsection{Lasso: Infinite Violation}
\label{sec:lasso}

The $\ell_1$ penalty in Lasso regression produces an extreme form of dominance: among a group of correlated features, it selects exactly one and sets all others' coefficients to zero.

Under the iterative optimizer framework (Definition~\ref{def:iterative}), the selected feature $d(f)$ receives all attribution ($\varphi_{d(f)}(f) > 0$) while $\varphi_k(f) = 0$ for all other $k$ in the same group.
By surjectivity---which holds because regularization path tie-breaking and data perturbation can make any feature the selected one---the Rashomon property is satisfied.

The attribution ratio is infinite: $\varphi_{d(f)}/\varphi_k = \infty$ for $k \neq d(f)$.
This is the most severe possible violation, and it holds at \emph{any} correlation $\rho > 0$.

\subsection{Neural Networks: Conditional Violation}
\label{sec:nn}

Neural networks with random initialization exhibit a form of symmetry breaking analogous to the \GBDT{} first-mover effect.
When correlated features compete for gradient flow early in training, the feature that receives the largest initial weight perturbation ``captures'' the correlated signal, accumulating higher attribution through subsequent epochs.

Formally, we model this via a captured-feature function $d(f)$ satisfying the iterative optimizer axioms \emph{conditional on the initialization inducing a dominance structure}.
The dominance condition---that $\varphi_{d(f)}(f) > \varphi_k(f)$ for $k \neq d(f)$ in the same group---holds when the initialization asymmetry is not erased by regularization or early stopping.
Surjectivity follows from the symmetry of standard initialization distributions (e.g., Kaiming, Xavier): each feature has equal probability of receiving the largest initial weight.

The impossibility therefore holds conditionally: whenever a neural network's training dynamics produce a dominant feature per correlated group, the Attribution Impossibility applies.
The ratio is architecture- and initialization-dependent rather than closed-form, but the qualitative conclusion is the same.

\subsection{Random Forests: Bounded Violation (Contrast)}
\label{sec:rf}

Random forests provide a revealing contrast.
Because each tree is trained independently on a bootstrap sample, there is no shared residual stream---feature $j$ being selected in tree $t$ does not reduce feature $k$'s signal in tree $t+1$.

The expected split count per feature within a group of size $m$ is $T/m$ for all features, with deviations of order $O(\sqrt{T})$ from bootstrap sampling noise.
The attribution ratio is therefore
\[
    \frac{\varphi_{j_1}(f)}{\varphi_k(f)} = 1 + O(1/\sqrt{T}) \;\to\; 1 \quad \text{as } T \to \infty.
\]
The Rashomon property may hold for finite $T$ (different bootstrap samples can produce different feature orderings), so the qualitative impossibility may apply.
But the quantitative violation \emph{vanishes} with more trees---the opposite of the \GBDT{} regime, where more rounds compound the first-mover advantage.

This contrast is the key structural insight: sequential residual-sharing creates divergent inequity, while independent aggregation creates convergent equity.
\DASH{} exploits precisely this distinction.

\section{Resolution: Ensemble Attribution via \DASH}
\label{sec:resolution}

The impossibility arises from sequential dependence: a single model's iterative optimization creates a dominant feature, and different random seeds create different dominant features.
\DASH{} resolves this by averaging attributions across an ensemble of $M$ independently trained models.

\begin{definition}[\DASH{} consensus attribution]
\label{def:dash}
Given models $f_1, \ldots, f_M$ trained from independent seeds, the \emph{consensus attribution} for feature $j$ is
$\bar{\varphi}_j = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{i=1}^{M} \varphi_j(f_i)$.
An ensemble is \emph{balanced} if, within each group, every feature serves as first-mover in exactly $M/m$ models.
\end{definition}

\begin{corollary}[\DASH{} achieves equity]
\label{cor:equity}
For a balanced ensemble and any two features $j, k$ in the same group $\ell$,
$\bar{\varphi}_j = \bar{\varphi}_k$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
By balance, $j$ is first-mover in $M/m$ models and non-first-mover in $(m-1)M/m$ models.
By Axiom~\ref{ax:splits} and~\ref{ax:proportional}, the total attribution for $j$ is
$\sum_i \varphi_j(f_i) = c \cdot \bigl[\tfrac{M}{m} \cdot \tfrac{T}{2-\rho^2} + \tfrac{(m-1)M}{m} \cdot \tfrac{(1-\rho^2)T}{2-\rho^2}\bigr]$.
This expression is identical for every feature in the group, so $\bar{\varphi}_j = \bar{\varphi}_k$.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Between-group stability.}
Under independence of seeds, $\Var(\bar{\varphi}_j) = \Var(\varphi_j) / M$, which vanishes as $M \to \infty$.
The between-group ranking therefore stabilizes: features in groups with higher true signal are consistently ranked above those with lower signal, with convergence rate $O(1/M)$.

\paragraph{Within-group completeness.}
Since $\bar{\varphi}_j = \bar{\varphi}_k$ for same-group features, the consensus ranking produces a \emph{tie} rather than an arbitrary ordering.
This relaxation of completeness parallels Arrow's resolution: when individual ``voters'' (models) disagree on the ordering of symmetric alternatives, the correct aggregation is indifference.
The tie is not a deficiency---it faithfully reflects the DGP symmetry.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Attribution impossibility results.}
\citet{bilodeau2024impossibility} prove that completeness and linearity cannot coexist for feature attributions.
\citet{huang2024failings} show SHAP values can misrank features, though only in Boolean settings.
\citet{srinivas2019full} prove that complete attributions cannot be weakly input-dependent, and \citet{rao2025limits} establishes Kolmogorov complexity barriers to explainability.
Our result is the first to address cross-model \emph{stability}, provide quantitative architecture-dependent bounds on the severity of violation, and offer a constructive resolution.

\paragraph{Rashomon effect and model multiplicity.}
\citet{laberge2023partial} study feature attribution consensus across Rashomon sets, concluding that rankings are inherently partial orders.
\citet{rudin2024amazing} advocate embracing model multiplicity rather than selecting a single ``best'' model.
Our theorem formalizes the consequences: the partial order structure is not an artifact of insufficient search but a mathematical inevitability under collinearity, and \DASH{} provides a principled aggregation over the Rashomon set.

\paragraph{Fairness impossibility.}
\citet{chouldechova2017fair} and \citet{kleinberg2017inherent} prove that calibration, balance, and equal false positive rates cannot coexist when base rates differ.
Our result has the same trilemma structure---faithfulness, stability, and completeness cannot coexist under collinearity---making the parallel between fairness and explainability impossibilities explicit for the first time.

\paragraph{Formal verification for ML.}
\citet{nipkow2009social} formalized Arrow's theorem in Isabelle/HOL, demonstrating that social choice impossibilities benefit from machine verification.
\citet{zhang2026statistical} recently brought statistical learning theory into Lean~4.
Our work contributes the first formally verified result in explainable AI, with 29 theorems compiled against Mathlib.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Our proof axiomatizes gradient boosting at the level of split-count formulas rather than deriving them from algorithmic definitions; the axioms are justified by Gaussian conditioning arguments verified in SymPy, but extending the formalization to derive axioms from first principles remains open.
The impossibility requires non-negligible collinearity ($\rho > 0$); the practical impact is minor when $\rho$ is small, as the attribution ratio approaches 1.
The balanced ensemble assumption is idealized---finite ensembles may not achieve exact balance, though the $O(1/M)$ convergence is robust to approximate balance.
The variance bound (Corollary~1(b) in the Lean formalization) is stated but not yet formally proved, pending measure-theoretic infrastructure in Mathlib.

\paragraph{Regulatory implications.}
The EU AI Act (Art.~13(3)(b)(ii)) requires disclosure of ``known and foreseeable circumstances'' affecting system accuracy \citep{euaiact2024}.
Our theorem proves that feature attribution instability under collinearity is precisely such a circumstance: it is foreseeable (it occurs whenever correlated features are present), quantifiable ($1/(1-\rho^2)$ for \GBDT{}), and affects the accuracy of model explanations.
As CEN-CENELEC JTC~21 drafts harmonised standards for AI transparency, this class of limitation warrants explicit acknowledgment.

\paragraph{Open problems.}
Three directions merit further investigation.
First, characterize all three relaxation paths of the trilemma: we resolve via relaxing completeness, but what is achievable by relaxing faithfulness or stability instead?
Second, derive the split-count axioms from algorithmic definitions of gradient boosting within Lean~4, closing the gap between axiomatic and algorithmic formalization.
Third, generalize the collinearity condition beyond linear correlation to mutual information $I(X_j; X_k) > 0$, which would extend the impossibility to nonlinear dependencies.
