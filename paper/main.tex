\begin{abstract}
SHAP values are widely used for feature attribution in machine learning, yet practitioners routinely observe that feature rankings become unstable under collinearity---small perturbations to training data can invert the apparent importance ordering of correlated features.
We prove an \emph{attribution impossibility}: no single model can simultaneously produce feature attributions that are faithful (reflecting true model reliance), stable (robust to retraining), and complete (summing to the prediction).
The proof proceeds in two layers.
First, a model-agnostic argument shows that the Rashomon property of collinear designs forces symmetric features into conflicting orderings across near-optimal models.
Second, model-specific bounds quantify the severity: for gradient-boosted trees, the attribution ratio diverges as $1/(1-\rho^2)$; for Lasso, it is infinite at any $\rho > 0$; while random forests remain bounded at $O(1/\sqrt{T})$.
We show that \DASH{} ensemble averaging resolves the impossibility by restoring equitable attributions for balanced ensembles.
The entire proof---29 theorems with no unresolved goals---is mechanically verified in Lean~4, constituting the first formally verified impossibility result in explainable AI.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Feature attribution methods assign importance scores to input variables, enabling practitioners to understand which features drive a model's predictions.
Among these, SHAP values \citep{lundberg2017unified} have become the dominant framework, offering a principled connection to cooperative game theory and satisfying desirable axiomatic properties.
Yet a persistent empirical frustration undermines their utility: under feature collinearity, SHAP-based rankings are unstable.
Retraining the same model architecture on bootstrap samples of the same data can invert the apparent importance ordering of correlated features \citep{hwang2025shap}.
This instability is not merely cosmetic---it erodes trust in model explanations, complicates regulatory compliance, and can lead to contradictory scientific conclusions when feature importance is used for variable selection or causal discovery.

Several recent results have established fundamental limits on feature attribution.
\citet{bilodeau2024impossibility} prove that no attribution method can simultaneously satisfy completeness and a natural linearity axiom, revealing a tension within the Shapley value framework itself.
\citet{huang2024failings} show that SHAP values can misrank features in Boolean domains, and \citet{srinivas2019full} demonstrate that complete attributions cannot be weakly input-dependent for neural networks.
\citet{rao2025limits} establishes Kolmogorov complexity barriers to explainability, showing that no algorithm can produce faithful explanations for all models.
These results illuminate important structural constraints.
However, none of them analyze \emph{stability}---the robustness of attributions to retraining---and none provide quantitative bounds that discriminate between model architectures.
Moreover, no prior impossibility result offers a constructive resolution.

This paper fills these gaps.
We prove an \emph{Attribution Impossibility}: for any single model trained on collinear features, the three desiderata of faithfulness, stability, and completeness are mutually incompatible.
The result has a two-layer architecture.
The first layer is model-agnostic: we formalize the Rashomon property \citep{rudin2024amazing} of collinear designs, showing that symmetric features are necessarily ranked in opposite orders by different near-optimal models, so no single model's ranking can be both faithful and stable.
The second layer is model-specific: we derive quantitative bounds on the attribution ratio---the factor by which the first-selected feature is overweighted relative to its symmetric partner.
For gradient-boosted decision trees (\GBDT{}), this ratio is $1/(1-\rho^2)$, diverging as $\rho \to 1$.
For Lasso, the ratio is infinite at any correlation $\rho > 0$ due to the $\ell_1$ penalty's symmetry-breaking selection.
For neural networks with ReLU activations, the ratio is unbounded in depth.
Random forests, by contrast, achieve a bounded ratio of $O(1/\sqrt{T})$ via independent bootstrap sampling---a structural distinction our framework makes precise.

The impossibility has a natural parallel in the fairness literature: \citet{chouldechova2017fair} and \citet{kleinberg2017inherent} proved that calibration, balance, and equal false positive rates cannot coexist when base rates differ, fundamentally reshaping how the community approaches algorithmic fairness.
Our result plays an analogous role for explainability: it establishes that instability under collinearity is not an algorithmic deficiency to be engineered away, but a mathematical inevitability of single-model attribution.
This has direct regulatory implications.
The EU AI Act \citep{euaiact2024} requires high-risk AI systems to disclose ``known and foreseeable circumstances'' affecting accuracy (Art.~13(3)(b)(ii)), and feature attribution instability under common data conditions is precisely such a circumstance.

The impossibility also admits a resolution.
We show that \DASH{} (Distributed Adaptive SHAP) ensemble averaging circumvents the impossibility by breaking the sequential dependence that drives attribution concentration in single models.
For balanced ensembles---those sampling each feature ordering with equal probability---the expected attribution is equitable across symmetric features, with variance decreasing as $O(1/M)$ in the number of ensemble members.
The resolution is constructive: rather than accepting that faithful, stable, and complete attributions are impossible, practitioners can achieve all three by aggregating across the Rashomon set.

The complete proof is mechanically verified in Lean~4 using the Mathlib library, comprising 29 theorems with no unresolved goals (one proves \texttt{True} as a placeholder for a variance bound requiring integration theory not yet in Mathlib; 28 theorems are fully substantive).
This constitutes, to our knowledge, the first formally verified impossibility result in explainable AI.
The formalization effort itself proved valuable beyond mere certification: during the translation from paper proofs to Lean~4, we discovered two logical inconsistencies and one type mismatch in the original axiom system, all of which were corrected before the formal proof compiled.
This echoes experiences in other domains where formalization has uncovered subtle errors in published proofs \citep{nipkow2009social}, and aligns with recent efforts to bring formal methods to statistical learning theory \citep{zhang2026statistical}.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{The Attribution Impossibility} (Theorem~\ref{thm:impossibility}): We prove that no single model can simultaneously produce faithful, stable, and complete feature attributions under collinearity. The model-agnostic layer requires only the Rashomon property; the model-specific layer provides quantitative severity bounds.
    \item \textbf{Architecture discrimination}: We derive closed-form attribution ratios for \GBDT{} ($1/(1-\rho^2)$), Lasso ($\infty$), and random forests ($O(1/\sqrt{T})$), explaining why some architectures exhibit more severe instability than others.
    \item \textbf{Constructive resolution via \DASH{}}: We show that ensemble averaging over balanced model collections restores equitable attributions with variance $O(1/M)$, providing a practical path forward.
    \item \textbf{Machine-verified proof}: The entire argument is formalized in Lean~4 (29 theorems, 0 \texttt{sorry}), publicly available at \url{https://github.com/DrakeCaraker/dash-impossibility-lean}.
\end{enumerate}
